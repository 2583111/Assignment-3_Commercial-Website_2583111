The use of data journalism has transformed the provision of information and storytelling in the digital era, which allows journalists to use the power that is provided by data to communicate factual, compelling, and complex information to readers in visually simple, efficient, and effective ways. The core of this transformation is the use of data visualization, which has become a considerable tool for creating narratives that are not only informative but also engaging, efficient, and accessible to diverse audiences. The term data visualization is core focus in this essay but what is it, what is my understanding on it, when looking at the reading "Overview of Data Visualization" to help form my understanding, two definitions resonated with me, the first by Manovich (2010), “a transformation of quantified data which is not visual into a visual representation” (Qi Li, 2020) and the second by Bikakis (2018) who defines it as a "presentation of data in a pictorial or graphical format" (Qi Li, 2020), these explanations both helped my understanding, specifically the point of translating data to a visual form, I also appreciate Bikakis (2018) further detailing the objective of data visualization as it "provides users with intuitive means to interactively explore and analyse data, enabling them to effectively identify interesting patterns, infer correlations and causalities, and supports sense-making activities" (Qi Li, 2020). This helped form my working definition of data visualizations as a form of translating complex data and representing it in visual form to effectively and efficiently help allow for quicker interpretation and easier communication to users.

The subject up for analysis is a piece by The Guardian titled “A third of Americans are already facing above-average warming,” which was published more than 2 years ago, roughly during 2021. The message the article wants to convey is that a large portion of the U.S. population is experiencing an above-average temperature increase, "with 499 counties already breaching 1.5C (2.7F) of heating." (Anon., 2022) This significant warming, driven by climate change, is unevenly distributed across the country, with hotspots emerging in areas like California to the northeast. The article highlights the severe impacts of this warming, including increased heatwaves, wildfires, and permafrost melting, which are reshaping local environments and communities. The situation highlights the urgent need for global efforts to limit further temperature rises and mitigate climate-related risks.

The piece uses two forms of data visualization: a bar chart, which is used to compare categorical data; the one in the article uses it to count the U.S. population that is living in counties that have either warmed by 1.5C, warmed but less than 1.5C, or decreased, allowing for easy visual comparison as the bars are stacked.

Additionally, choropleth maps are used to illustrate geographic data across the U.S. The map uses a divergent or bipolar progression/scale to represent the different temperature data values across different counties, which provides an intuitive visual representation of spatial differences. The rationale behind using these forms of data visualization, the choropleth map and bar charts, is that both effectively represent the data and are easily readable.

The bar chart is ideal for comparing categories as well as variables within categories. Even with stacking within the data found in a bar, they still provide a clear visual distinction between groups. The choropleth map has the ability to convey complex geographic data in a simple, visually appealing format; this is done by color-coding the counties based on temperature data between the years. The map makes it easy for readers to identify patterns, such as areas with higher values using dark red/maroon and lower values using a light blue. This colour difference is important, as stated in "Principles of Effective Data Visualization," that "colour represents information, whether in a direct and obvious way," (Midway, 2020) and "colour will often be the first visual information a reader gets, and with this knowledge, colour should be strategically used to amplify your visual message, whether in an indirect and subtle way," (Midway, 2020) and by having it color-coded it makes the data presentable and easy to interpret and readable. The use of the choropleth map is particularly suitable for data with a geographic component, as it provides a direct visual link between the data and the physical locations it represents. The use of these data visualization forms ensures that the data is not only accessible but also engaging, as it enhances the reader's ability to quickly and accurately interpret the information.

I believe interactivity plays a crucial role in enhancing user engagement within data visualization pieces. In the article, the interactive elements are tied to both data visualization forms; this is effective as it allows users to explore the data dynamically, providing for an experience that can significantly help deepen the users understanding of the data. The choropleth map can be interacted with; particularly, the user is able to hover over different counties on the map to reveal more detailed data of the temperature, that of which was recorded in 1895, 2021, and the change/increase.

Additionally, the bar graph interactions lie within the bars, as when the user hovers over them, the data is presented more clearly, not only revealing the percentages but also the total number of people living in the county.

These kinds of forms of interactivity transform the user experience from a passive recipient of the information into an active participant, enabling them to engage with the data in a way that is both intuitive and engaging. Personally, I believe the interactive elements in the article are effective both in demonstration and execution. The data is represented well and is easily readable; then the ability to interact with the data furthers the user's engagement from passive reading to actively engaging, but it also deepens understanding of the complex information being presented. For example, being able to hover over different counties to see the specific values allows for a more nuanced interpretation of the data. I think this is particularly valuable in data journalism, where the goal is often to communicate intricate stories that require the reader to understand multiple layers of complex information. If I had a suggestion for improvement, it would be to add a feature that allows filtering data from any time after 1895, enhancing interactivity, and personalizing the experience for the user.

Reflecting on my experiences with data visualization, I realize how much it shapes the way I interact with and interpret data. It enables me to quickly grasp the effectiveness of what's being presented while also appreciating the complexity involved in creating clear and understandable visualizations. I understand how challenging it can be to convey complex information effectively while ensuring it's accessible to all users. This highlights the importance of designing interactive visualizations that cater to users with varying levels of understanding, making the data accessible to a broad audience.

The article effectively applies User Interface (UI) and User Experience (UX) design principles, which is crucial for enhancing data visualization. The clean and uncluttered visual elements allow users to focus on the data; this can be seen through the colour scheme used, as it is a divergent scale that intuitively represents temperature differences—warm colours indicating higher temperatures and cool colours indicating lower ones. This design choice facilitates quick comprehension and immediate understanding of the data's meaning, which is essential for effective communication, as stated in "The Basic Principles of User Interface Design." It's important to strive for clarity as to "Avoid anything that confuses people or doesn’t help them interact." (Anon., 2024). Another strength is the flexibility of the data visualization elements as they scale to different devices, following another principle: "Create a UI that will work and look great across multiple platforms." (Anon., 2024). The bar graphs have exceptional flexibility as they change orientation as well.

The only other issue is that the interactivity from the Choropleth maps is removed as there is no mouse to hover, and an element displays additional information. Despite these strengths, there are areas where the UI/UX design could be improved. An example I stated early was to add a feature to filter data by specific years after 1895, which would enhance user interactivity and engagement by having a personalized experience, which would deepen their understanding of the data presented.

UI/UX design significantly influences the user’s behaviour and understanding. A well-designed interface encourages users to engage more with the data, leading to a greater comprehension of the information. The article demonstrates how UI/UX design plays a pivotal role in ensuring that data is not only accessible but also engaging and informative.

The Guardian’s article excels in data-driven storytelling by integrating data visualizations like the choropleth map and bar chart with a compelling narrative on climate change's impact. These visualizations are both informative and emotionally engaging, with interactive elements allowing for engaging exploration of the data. The narrative provides crucial context, making complex data more relatable and easier to understand. The emotional impact is amplified through colour choices, effectively communicating the seriousness of the issue. This article highlights the importance of interactivity, narrative, and emotional design in creating impactful, accessible data visualizations that both inform and engage audiences.
